Software Engineer ILearn & Ship

SE I engineers are typically in their first one to two years. We don't hire them to fail safely — we hire them to ship real work with strong scaffolding. Skills carry the domain expertise; SE I brings judgment and curiosity. The handbook removes the variance.

Software Engineer IIApply & Own

SE II owns features end-to-end. They configure projects, choose skills, and ship without supervision. Their PRs are reviewed for judgment, not for catching basic mistakes — the skills caught those.

Software Engineer IIIDefine & Mentor

SE III shapes patterns for the team. They write new skills when none exist. They mentor SE I and II by example — through PRs, through pairing, through the decisions they document.

Software Engineer IVArchitect & Influence

SE IV operates above features. They design systems, set technical direction, and own the org-wide standards in the handbook itself. They're the ones who notice when a pattern needs to become a skill — and they write it.

Software Engineer I

The first eighteen months.

SE I is where the handbook earns its keep. Done right, an SE I in their third week ships at a quality bar that took a SE III five years to reach on a team without skills.

SE I
Section 3 · Learn & Ship

The Software Engineer I role

12–18 months at this level. Tightly scaffolded. Heavily mentored. Real work, not training-wheel work.

◆ What SE I owns
  • Tasks scoped by a Tech Lead or SE III — the SE I doesn't yet scope their own work.
  • Small features and bug fixes — always with a plan, never freehand.
  • Test coverage for their own work — they don't ship code without tests, and they don't ship tests that don't fail under failure conditions.
  • Documentation updates that touch the code they wrote — if you changed the API, you updated the doc.
  • Reviewing other SE I PRs as a learning exercise — under supervision, with a SE II or above co-reviewing.
✓ Mandatory skills
  • /quick-plan before every task — required. No editor opens before a plan exists, even for a copy change.
  • /quick-work to execute plans — required for any task that has a /quick-plan.
  • /code-review before every PR — required. The Tech Lead checks the output is attached; PRs without it bounce.
  • /sec-audit on any auth, payments, or PII code — required. No exceptions for "small changes."
  • /smart-commit for commit messages — required. Consistency across the team starts here.
  • /feature-plan on any work bigger than a single session — required. Pair with a senior to review the plan before implementation.
  • /find-fix when diagnosing a bug — required before planning the fix. Cause first, fix second.
  • /explain when reading unfamiliar code — recommended. Strongly preferred over guessing what code does.
  • /test-gen when adding tests to a new file — recommended. Pairs naturally with /quick-work.
⏱ A day in the life — Maya, SE I, week 3
9:00 AM — Maya logs in. Her Jira board shows three tickets assigned: a UI bug (logo alignment on mobile dashboard), a copy change in the welcome email, and a flaky test in the auth suite. She doesn't open her editor yet. 9:05 AM — She runs /quick-plan on the first ticket — the UI bug. Output: "Task: Fix logo alignment on mobile dashboard. Files: 1. Steps: 3. Estimate: S." She skim-reads it in 20 seconds, agrees, and replies B (implement now). 9:15 AM — Standup. Priya, the Tech Lead, asks about the auth flaky test. Maya hasn't started it yet but doesn't fake a status. "I'll plan it after the UI fix — likely M-sized. If it grows, I'll flag." Priya nods. That answer would have been "yeah, looking at it" two weeks ago. The handbook taught her not to bluff. 9:20 AM — Back to the dashboard fix. /quick-work made the change. Maya verifies in browser — desktop and mobile, on three viewport sizes. Logo is aligned. She runs /code-review on the diff. Output flags one issue: a missing accessibility label on the new icon she touched. She fixes it and re-runs review. Clean. 9:45 AM — /smart-commit generates the commit message: "fix(ui): align logo on mobile dashboard." She pushes. PR opens. Priya approves within the hour. 10:30 AM — On to the welcome email copy change. Trivially S. She almost skips /quick-plan and... no, wait. The handbook says every task gets a plan. She runs it. 30 seconds. Output: "Task: Update welcome email subject line. Files: 2. Steps: 1." Two files. The plan caught that the copy lives in two places — the email template and the i18n catalog. Without the plan, she would have shipped half the change. The plan paid for itself. 11:00 AM — The auth flaky test. /find-fix first — diagnose before plan. Output: "Root cause: race condition between mocked OAuth callback and session-cookie setter. The test setup doesn't await the session ID before clicking 'continue.'" Now she runs /quick-plan against the diagnosed cause. M-sized. She implements with /quick-work. 11:45 AM — She runs the test thirty times to verify it no longer flakes. Verify, don't trust — Principle 5. It passes thirty times. She pushes. 12:30 PM — Lunch. She doesn't think about work for an hour. 2:00 PM — PR review. Daniyal (SE II) asked her to review a small refactor PR. She runs /explain on a chunk of code she doesn't fully understand — a custom hook for paginated queries. The skill explains it in plain language. She leaves three comments on the PR, two of which Daniyal acts on. 3:00 PM — Pairs with Sahil, another SE I who started last week. She walks him through /quick-plan on his first ticket. He keeps trying to skip it. "No — every task. Even copy changes. I learned that the hard way this morning." 4:30 PM — Ends the day with a small doc PR: she noticed the README for the auth module was out of date after her flaky-test fix. /smart-commit generates the message: "docs(auth): update OAuth flow diagram after race-condition fix." Pushed. 5:30 PM — Logs off. She didn't write a single line of code today without first running a plan. That's the standard, not the exception.
✕ Anti-patterns — what SE I must not do
  • Open the editor without running /quick-plan (or /feature-plan for larger work). The plan is the entry condition for coding.
  • Ship a PR without /code-review output attached. Reviewer rejects on sight — they don't run it for you.
  • Bypass /sec-audit on regulated code "because it's a small change." The size of the change has nothing to do with the size of the security risk.
  • Trust AI-generated code without verifying acceptance criteria. Read the diff. Run the tests. Click the button. Don't assume.
  • Accept blame for a production issue without diagnosis. Run /find-fix. The bug has a cause; the cause is not "I was sloppy."
  • Stay silent in standup when blocked. Asking for help is the cheapest cost-reduction mechanism the team has — silence costs everyone.
  • Copy-paste code from a teammate's PR without running /explain on it. Borrowed patterns become bugs when their context isn't borrowed too.
↗ Promotion signal — from SE I to SE II
  • Consistent skill usage for 3+ months — every task, no exceptions, even when nobody's watching.
  • 95%+ PRs merge on first review — the review catches judgment calls, not basic mistakes.
  • Caught a real bug via /sec-audit or /dep-vuln — something a senior reviewer would have missed without the skill.
  • Wrote a /decision-doc for an architecture choice that affected someone other than themselves.
  • Mentored another SE I on skill usage during onboarding — not just answered questions, but actively coached.
Note — The promotion to SE II is not about more code per week. It's about the moment a Tech Lead realizes they no longer need to scope your tickets. You're scoping them yourself, and the scopes are right.
Software Engineer II

Autonomy, with proof.

SE II is the first level where you scope your own work. The reviews change shape too — they get faster, but the questions get sharper.

SE II
Section 4 · Apply & Own

The Software Engineer II role

18–30 months at this level. Owns features end-to-end. Configures projects. Mentors SE I by example.

◆ What SE II owns
  • Features end-to-end — no daily Tech Lead scoping needed. You read the requirement, you scope it, you ship it.
  • Project-level .heaptrace/ configurations — including quick-plan.json, code-review.json, and code-standards.json for the projects you touch most.
  • Test strategy for your own features — what to test at unit level, what at integration, what at e2e. The strategy is documented in the PR.
  • Onboarding documentation for components you touch — when an SE I joins, your docs are part of how they learn.
  • Pairing with SE I on review — not just rubber-stamp, but actual review with explanation.
  • Identifying patterns that should become skills — and raising them with SE III or above.
✓ Mandatory skills
  • All SE I mandates remain — they don't graduate; they compound.
  • /feature-plan and /feature-work for any multi-session feature work. No exceptions for "I've built something like this before."
  • /sec-audit self-initiated — not just when reminded. If your code touches a security surface, you run it before opening the PR.
  • /test-gen for new test suites — paired with manual review of the generated tests for hallucination patterns.
  • /code-standards per-project tuning — every project you touch should have its .heaptrace/ set up by the time you've shipped two features in it.
  • /message-craft for any cross-team or client-facing message — non-negotiable for client-facing, strongly recommended for internal.
  • /release-notes when your work ships in a release — your features get documented by you, not by the PM.
  • /decision-doc when you make a non-trivial technical choice — the doc lives forever; the Slack message doesn't.
  • /arch-review on architecture changes you propose — even if you're confident, the skill catches things confidence misses.
⏱ A day in the life — Daniyal, SE II, 18 months in
8:45 AM — Daniyal opens the new project repo he was assigned last Friday. It's a payments-integration feature, three to four weeks of work. Before he opens any file, he runs /code-standards on the repo. The skill detects the project uses React Query — but the existing rules are tuned for SWR. He updates .heaptrace/code-standards.json to match. Commits it as the first PR on this project. 9:00 AM — /feature-plan for the payments integration. He spends the next 40 minutes reading the requirement, running the plan, reviewing the output, iterating once. The plan comes out with: 12 sub-tasks, 4 risk items (one of which Daniyal hadn't considered — a webhook idempotency edge case), data-flow diagram, acceptance criteria broken down per sub-task. 9:40 AM — He shares the plan with Sarah (SE III) for a 5-minute review. She catches one thing: a misalignment between his retry strategy and the team's existing convention for Stripe webhooks. He updates the plan and adds a /decision-doc capturing the retry strategy so the next person who touches webhooks finds it. 10:00 AM — Standup. He gives a precise update: "Yesterday: finished the user-invite redesign, merged. Today: scoping the payments integration, plan reviewed by Sarah, expecting first PR by Wednesday. Blockers: none. Next: I'll need 30 min from Marcus on Wednesday to walk through the webhook idempotency edge." 10:15 AM — Two SE I PRs in his queue. The first looks clean — /code-review output attached, tests pass, plan referenced. Approved in 8 minutes. The second is from Sahil — he forgot to run /code-review. Daniyal bounces it. "Run /code-review and re-submit — I don't run it for you." No softening. The next time Sahil submits a PR, the skill output is attached. The bounce taught it. 11:00 AM — Starts implementation on sub-task 1 of the payments plan. /feature-work generates the scaffolding. He hand-writes the parts that involve the Stripe SDK, because he doesn't trust generated code on third-party SDK calls without verification — Principle 5 again. 12:30 PM — Lunch. 1:30 PM — Continues implementation. Hits the webhook idempotency edge mentioned in the plan. Spends 45 minutes on it, ends up with a clean solution. Adds a test for it (the test fails when he deliberately breaks the idempotency check — verified, not assumed). 2:30 PM — Scope creep risk. The PM Slacks: "Hey, can we also add tipping support while you're in there?" Daniyal pulls up his plan, points to it, and replies: "Tipping wasn't in the original scope. Want me to estimate it as a separate ticket? If we add it to this one, ship date moves from next Wednesday to the following Monday." The PM picks the separate ticket. The plan paid for itself again. 3:00 PM — Runs /message-craft on a Slack post he's drafting for the PM channel about the scope change. The skill catches one hedge ("I think this might be...") and removes it. The message goes out clean. 4:00 PM — Pairs with Sarah (SE III) on a /decision-doc they're co-authoring — state management refactor for the dashboard. Daniyal does most of the writing; Sarah pushes back on the trade-off analysis section. He rewrites it twice. The doc lands before end of day. 5:00 PM — Last PR review of the day. Approves. Logs off. He shipped one sub-task, reviewed two SE I PRs, co-wrote a decision doc, and resisted scope creep. None of this required a Tech Lead to scaffold for him. That's the SE II shape.
✕ Anti-patterns — what SE II must not do
  • Re-do scaffolding work the skills already do. If you find yourself writing the same kind of plan from scratch every Monday, you're vibe-coding the plan — run the skill instead.
  • Skip /feature-plan when a ticket says "feature." Token waste? Sure. Quality matters more. Cynicism about the skill is what kills it on the team.
  • Leave .heaptrace/ unconfigured on a project you've shipped multiple features in. That forces every SE I after you to ask the same questions you already answered.
  • Rubber-stamp SE I PRs. Reviews are teaching. If you don't have time to explain why something is wrong, you don't have time to approve it.
  • Ship a feature without a /code-review-passing diff and a passing test suite. Standards have teeth — Principle 7. You're the one giving them teeth at SE II.
  • Hide a hallucination you caught. Every hallucination that's not reported is one that hits the next developer harder.
  • Accept a scope-creep request without re-running the plan. The plan is the negotiation document — bring it to the conversation.
↗ Promotion signal — from SE II to SE III
  • Owned 5+ features ship-to-prod without escalation — including at least one with cross-team dependencies.
  • Authored or significantly extended at least 1 internal skill — proven by it being used by someone other than you.
  • Mentored an SE I from onboarding to first solo feature — the SE I ships without escalating to you for help.
  • Drove a /decision-doc that changed team direction — not just documented; actually altered how the team works.
  • Identified an anti-pattern that became a handbook entry — caught a pattern, named it, escalated to SE III/IV for codification.
Note — The signal isn't tenure. We've promoted in 14 months and we've held in place at 30. The signal is that SE III work starts happening before the title catches up.
Software Engineer III

The pattern-setter.

SE III is the role that scales the team. They turn one person's hard-won knowledge into a skill the whole team gets to use forever.

SE III
Section 5 · Define & Mentor

The Software Engineer III role

24–48 months at this level. Owns patterns. Writes skills. Reviews architecture. Mentors SE I and II directly.

◆ What SE III owns
  • Patterns and standards within a domain — frontend, backend, mobile, data, infra. You're the one the team escalates "is this how we do it?" questions to.
  • Skill authorship and refinement — when no skill exists for a recurring need, you write it. When a skill is wrong, you fix it.
  • Architecture reviews via /arch-review — both for your own work and for cross-domain proposals from SE II.
  • Cross-feature consistency — you notice when two features diverge in shape and you reconcile them, before three features become four.
  • Direct mentorship of SE I and II — including running the first skip-level meetings to feed promotion signal back to the Tech Lead.
  • Quarterly /tech-debt-audit on your domain — the work nobody else will do if you don't.
✓ Mandatory skills
  • All SE I and II mandates remain — and you model them visibly so juniors see the standard.
  • /arch-review on any significant architecture change in your domain — your own or someone else's.
  • /tech-debt-audit quarterly per domain — calendar-blocked, not "when I have time."
  • /decision-doc for any pattern that affects others — the doc index is your responsibility, not the SE IV's.
  • /onboard-dev content updates when patterns change — old docs that contradict new patterns are how teams produce inconsistent PRs.
  • /pr-strategy ownership for your domain — branch hygiene, review SLAs, merge cadence. Set them, enforce them.
  • /perf-audit when shipping a feature that has scale implications — the SE II didn't catch it; you do.
  • /integration-plan when introducing a new external dependency — third-party SDK, new internal service, anything across a network boundary.
  • /sprint-plan co-ownership with the Tech Lead — you bring the domain knowledge into the sprint shape.
⏱ A day in the life — Sarah, SE III, 4 years at Heaptrace, owns Frontend Patterns
8:30 AM — Sarah starts the day with the quarterly /tech-debt-audit on the frontend component library. The skill flags 17 items. She spends 90 minutes triaging — 6 are real, 4 are noise, 7 are valid but already covered by an in-flight migration. She turns the 6 real ones into tickets and assigns them across the next two sprints. 10:00 AM — Daniyal (SE II) pings: he wants her review on a /decision-doc he's drafting about React Query vs SWR for the new dashboard. She doesn't just rubber-stamp it. She pulls up the existing decision doc from two years ago (her own), reads it, then reads Daniyal's draft. Catches that he's argued from a different context — they have different data freshness needs now. She suggests a small extension to the original doc rather than a new one, and a deprecation note on the legacy SWR usage. He agrees. She adds the revision herself in 10 minutes. 10:45 AM — Standup. Sarah reports: "Yesterday: tech-debt audit on the component library, 6 real items now ticketed. Today: skip- level with Maya at 11, then writing the legacy-migration skill I mentioned last week. Blockers: none." 11:00 AM — Skip-level 1:1 with Maya (SE I, week 3). Maya is nervous; this is her first one. Sarah keeps it casual. She asks what Maya is finding hard, what she's enjoying, where the handbook feels unclear. Maya mentions that /quick-plan sometimes feels excessive on trivial tickets — but then describes the welcome-email two-files incident from earlier in the week, and answers her own question mid-sentence. Sarah laughs and says "That's why." She makes a note: Maya is ahead of pace. Tell Priya at the next promotion review. 12:00 PM — Lunch with Marcus (SE IV). They talk about whether the new event-driven module he's designing should reuse the existing queueing infrastructure or stand up a new one. Sarah pushes for reuse; Marcus pushes for separation. They agree to schedule a /system-design review next week. Decision deferred, but defined. 1:30 PM — She writes a new internal skill: /legacy-migration-step. It's based on a pattern she's seen recur five times in the last quarter — a feature needs to be migrated from the legacy state store, but every developer does it slightly differently. By 3 PM the skill is drafted. She runs it on the next legacy-migration ticket in the backlog as a dogfooding test. Two issues caught, fixed. The skill ships to .heaptrace/skills/internal/ on Friday after one more review pass. 3:30 PM — Reviews an /arch-review output that another SE III in the backend domain ran on his proposed event-bus schema. She doesn't just check the syntax — she checks the architectural fit with the frontend's data subscriptions. Finds one mismatch (the event payloads don't include enough context for optimistic UI updates). Comments. The backend SE III replies within 20 minutes with a fix. 4:30 PM — Approves three PRs. One of them she bounces back — not because the code is wrong, but because the SE II author hadn't included the /decision-doc link for a non-trivial trade-off he made. "Add the doc. The next person needs to understand the choice." 5:15 PM — Logs off. She wrote no production code today. She wrote one skill, one decision revision, one tech-debt triage, and one mentorship hour. That's the SE III shape. The leverage is in the multipliers, not the keystrokes.
✕ Anti-patterns — what SE III must not do
  • Solve problems alone that should become skills or docs. One-off heroics make you look senior; they make the team weaker. Codify or step away.
  • Code-review only the syntax. SE III review is about architectural fit, pattern consistency, and risk. If your reviews are line-by-line nitpicks, you've regressed to SE II.
  • Skip the handbook update when a new pattern is adopted. Drift starts the day the doc lags the practice.
  • Hoard domain knowledge "because nobody asks." Push it out. /onboard-dev exists. The doc index exists. Use them.
  • Approve a PR that violates a pattern you wrote. That's how patterns die — slowly, by exception.
  • Treat mentorship as optional. Mentorship is the multiplier; without it, you're an expensive SE II.
  • Refuse to write a skill "because the team can just learn it." If you find yourself explaining the same thing three times, you've already wasted more time than writing the skill would have cost.
↗ Promotion signal — from SE III to SE IV
  • Authored 3+ skills used across multiple teams — proven by usage telemetry or by other SE IIIs citing them.
  • Drove a cross-domain architecture decision — frontend × backend, mobile × infra, the kind of decision that spans more than one SE III's domain.
  • Identified and resolved a recurring class of bugs — via a skill change or pattern change, not by fixing them one at a time.
  • Mentored an SE II to SE III promotion — the mentee shipped at SE III level for 3+ months before the title moved.
  • Influenced the handbook itself — a new principle, a new anti-pattern, a new doc section that carries your name in the contribution history.
Note — SE III to SE IV is the longest gap in the ladder. Most engineers who reach SE III stay there happily for years. That's not a failure mode — SE III is a genuinely high-leverage role. SE IV is the role for engineers who want to influence architecture across teams, not just within one.
Software Engineer IV (Staff)

The handbook itself.

SE IV is the role that owns this document. They notice the gap, name it, and write the principle. The team operates because they wrote down how.

SE IV
Section 6 · Architect & Influence

The Software Engineer IV (Staff) role

36+ months at this level, often indefinite. Operates above features. Designs systems. Owns org-wide standards.

◆ What SE IV owns
  • Org-wide engineering standards — the handbook itself, the principles, the doc structure. When this page changes, an SE IV signed off.
  • Cross-team architecture — systems that span multiple SE III domains. Event buses, data platforms, multi-service contracts.
  • The skill suite curriculum — which packs the team needs, which are missing, which are deprecated. The 101-skill inventory has an owner.
  • Mentorship of SE III — the longest mentorship loop in the company. Years, not quarters.
  • Engineering org-design input — hiring panels, promotion frameworks, headcount allocation. SE IV votes count.
  • External representation — the conference talk, the open-source PR upstream, the vendor evaluation that costs $200K to get wrong.
✓ Mandatory skills
  • All previous mandates remain — and SE IV models them publicly, in front of the org. If you skip your own skill, the org learns to.
  • /system-design for any new system or significant rearchitecture. The doc lives in the architecture index for 5+ years.
  • /integration-plan for cross-team work — the contract, the migration sequence, the rollback plan.
  • /cost-estimate on architectural choices — every architecture proposal includes the dollars-per-month projection at 1×, 10×, and 100× scale.
  • /scalability-review quarterly on owned systems — not "when something breaks," but on calendar.
  • /decision-doc archive ownership — the doc index, the patterns, the long arc. New SE IVs inherit the archive; they don't restart it.
  • /api-design for any new public or cross-service API — the schema is the contract; the contract is the constraint.
  • /db-design review on any new persistence layer — even if an SE III did the first pass.
  • /migration-plan for anything that touches production data — irreversible operations need a reversible plan.
⏱ A day in the life — Marcus, SE IV (Staff), 8 years at Heaptrace, owns Platform Architecture
7:30 AM — Marcus starts before standup. He's writing a /system-design doc for a new event-driven module the team needs in Q3. The doc is in its third revision. He spends 90 minutes on the failure modes section — the part most engineers skip and the part that pays off three years later. He includes a /cost-estimate sub-section showing the system's dollar cost at 1×, 10×, and 100× scale. The 100× number is the one that will determine whether they reuse Kafka or stand up something cheaper. He doesn't decide yet — he lets the doc make the case. 9:30 AM — Standup. He's deliberately brief. "Yesterday: system design draft 2 on the event module. Today: finishing the draft, review with Sarah at 4. Blockers: need a 30-min slot with the backend Tech Lead on the queueing reuse question." 9:45 AM — Reviews a /scalability-review Sarah (SE III) ran on the search service. She caught an indexing problem that would have surfaced at 5× current load. He approves her remediation plan with one addition: he asks her to write a /decision-doc on why they're not pre-emptively migrating to OpenSearch, because the next SE III who looks at this will ask. She agrees. 10:30 AM — Hiring panel. Two-hour slot for an SE III candidate. Marcus doesn't ask "tell me about a project" questions; he asks "walk me through a decision you made that turned out to be wrong, and what you'd do differently." The candidate's answer is honest and specific. He gives a strong yes. 12:30 PM — Lunch. Open-source PR upstream — he submits a patch to a library the team depends on. The maintainer responds within an hour. This is part of the job, not a side project. The library is critical infrastructure; a healthy upstream is risk reduction. 1:30 PM — Vendor evaluation call. An observability vendor is pitching. He runs /cost-estimate on their pricing model against the team's projected log volume for the next two years. The vendor's pricing is reasonable at year 1, brutal at year 3. He doesn't say no in the call; he says he'll review and reply by Friday. Then he replies on Wednesday so the vendor doesn't chase him. 2:30 PM — Handbook PR. He's updating Principle 4 (Output Consistency) with a new measurement framework — they've collected six months of data and one of the four metrics turned out to be noisier than expected. He's deprecating it and adding a replacement (reviewer-iteration delta on PRs above 200 lines). The PR has a long description; the change is small but the rationale is what the team will read. He merges to handbook-main after one review pass from another SE IV. 4:00 PM — System-design review with Sarah. She pushes back on the queueing-reuse decision. He has the cost-estimate numbers in his back pocket; he shares them. "At 10× scale, the new queue saves us $11K/month. At 1× it costs us a week of engineering. I think it's worth it." Sarah agrees. They schedule the migration plan for next sprint. 5:00 PM — Skip-level with one of the SE IIIs. Asks how the domain ownership is going, where the rough edges are, whether the quarterly tech-debt cadence is the right rhythm. The SE III gives honest feedback: the quarterly cadence is too slow for the mobile domain. Marcus agrees and proposes shifting it to monthly for mobile while keeping it quarterly elsewhere. Logged as a handbook PR for next week. 6:00 PM — Logs off. He wrote one system design, approved one scalability review, ran one hiring panel, contributed one upstream PR, evaluated one vendor, merged one handbook update, and ran one skip-level. Zero production code. That's the SE IV shape. The output is the architecture; the architecture is the team's velocity for the next three years.
✕ Anti-patterns — what SE IV must not do
  • Operate at SE III level. Reviewing PRs instead of designing systems is the most common SE IV failure mode. If your week is mostly PR queue, the org is missing an SE IV — even if your title says you have one.
  • Hoard architectural decisions in your head. The /decision-doc archive is the proof you exist. Without it, the moment you leave, the team forgets why anything is the way it is.
  • Skip the handbook PRs. The handbook is your job. Let the team drift on principle wording, and they drift on principle behavior six months later.
  • Treat SE IV as "senior senior." It's a distinct role. If you can't articulate what's different about it from SE III, the role isn't being staffed; the title is.
  • Take engineering work that an SE III could do. You're not respecting your team's growth path; you're consuming it. Hand the work to SE III; review the output.
  • Ship a system design without a cost estimate. Architecture without dollars is daydreaming. The dollars are part of the design.
  • Stay in the office. Conferences, upstream PRs, vendor calls, industry meetups — these are part of the job. The team's reputation outside the company is part of the architecture.
↗ Beyond SE IV
  • SE IV isn't the cap. The next step is Principal Engineer or Tech Lead. Both are scope expansions, not level expansions.
  • Principal Engineer — org-wide technical authority. Sets technical direction for the company, not the team. Reports into the CTO or directly to the board.
  • Tech Lead — cross-org leadership. Same depth as SE IV, but spans multiple teams (frontend + backend + mobile + infra). Trades some hands-on architecture for organizational leverage.
  • SE IV is the foundation for both paths. Without it, neither is open. The mistake we've watched repeatedly: promoting straight from SE III to Principal. It almost always fails. The handbook authorship loop only forms at SE IV.
  • Most SE IVs stay SE IV. That's a healthy outcome, not a stalled one. SE IV is where the deepest technical work lives. Not everyone wants the org-leverage trade.
Note — If you're an SE IV reading this and you've never updated the handbook, that's the gap. Pick a section. Improve it. The doc is yours to keep honest.
Cross-role

What everyone does, regardless of level.

These don't change with title. They're the floor. The SE IV runs them the same way the SE I does — and they bounce PRs that don't.

Universal Operating Norms · All Engineering Roles
  • Every PR runs /code-review — no exceptions, no "small change" carve-outs, no "I already eyeballed it." The output is attached to the PR description. PRs without it bounce on first review.
  • Every regulated change runs /sec-audit — auth, payments, PII, healthcare data, financial calculations. The skill output is attached. Any findings are resolved or explicitly accepted in writing in the PR description.
  • Every commit uses /smart-commit — for consistency across the team. The five-minute saving from skipping it costs the next developer twenty minutes when they're spelunking history six months later.
  • Every cross-team message considers /message-craft — required for client-facing communication, strongly recommended for internal. The skill catches premature apology, hedging, and scope creep before send.
  • Every incident is logged via /incident-response (for live response) or /incident-plan (for post-mortem and prevention). No incident is "too small to write up." The patterns only emerge from the log.
  • Every project has a .heaptrace/ folder — configured by SE II or above. quick-plan.json, code-review.json, code-standards.json at minimum. New projects are blocked from merging to main until the folder exists.
  • Every plan is reviewed by another human in 30 seconds before coding starts — even the SE IV asks a peer to skim the system design before they start prototyping. Plans that take longer than 30 seconds to read are rewritten.
  • Every hallucination caught gets reported back to the skill — via issue or PR. A hallucination that's not reported is one that will catch the next developer.
  • Every standup update follows the four-label format: Done · In Progress · Blockers · Next. No paragraphs. Everyone reads it the same way.
  • Every client-facing deliverable matches internal quality — same PR description structure, same plan rigor, same verification standard. If it's not good enough internally, it's not going to a client.
Promotion framework

What it takes to move up.

Time at level is the floor, not the ceiling. We've promoted faster and we've held longer. The signal that matters is when the next-level work starts happening before the title catches up.

Transition Time at level Skill mastery Mentorship Decision scope
SE I → SE II 12–18 mo All SE I mandates internalized — no reminders needed Mentees: 0; reviewing other SE I work under supervision Tickets, no scoping — scope comes from Tech Lead
SE II → SE III 18–30 mo Skill author or significant extender; /decision-doc routinely used Mentees: 1 SE I — through to first solo feature Feature scoping — owns end-to-end, no daily check-ins
SE III → SE IV 24–48 mo 3+ skills authored, used across teams; /arch-review ownership Mentees: 2 SE II — at least one to SE III promotion Cross-domain — frontend × backend, etc.
SE IV → Principal 36 mo+ Skill curriculum owner; handbook contributor Mentees: 2+ SE III; org-wide influence Org-wide — sets technical direction

Promotion is a confirmation of work already happening — not a permission slip to start. Every promotion in our history followed at least three months of next-level output before the title moved.
